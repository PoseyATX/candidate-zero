How to Play

A roguelike of Texas retail politics. Easy to learn; the county takes years.

The Shape of It

You are nobody, running for the Texas House. Eight weeks to the primary, six more to the general if you survive it. Before any of that counts, you must make the ballot by the end of week 8 — or the primary goes on without you.

Your Week

Each week you get 2 action points (AP) and a hand of cards. A card costs AP — sometimes money, volunteers, momentum, or a favor besides. Play until you're out of actions, then End Week. Every week your deck grows by one card, and when the race escalates you'll draft one of three new cards into your pool. Choices bind: your persona, issue, district, and region are locked at filing.

Reading a Card

  • The seal is the cost. Stamped tags beneath it are additional costs.
  • The dot and letters in the footer are the risk class.
  • p≈ and the meter show your odds of a clean result, given your state right now.
  • Dimmed cards can't be played yet — the small red line says why. They stay visible so you can plan for them.
  • CAMP stamps mark ballot-access actions always available before filing.
  • SHOP stamps mark asset buys (0 AP) — spend money or volunteers, keep your actions.
  • The corner seal and the card's tint mark its family — a bargain, an ally, an item, a liability. Plain cards are just plays. The game won't tell you a card is a trap; the tint says what kind of thing it is, and the words say the rest. Learn to read both.

Risk

  • SAFE — never a disaster. The floor is a wasted afternoon.
  • STD — honest work with honest variance.
  • VOL — swings big both ways, and the disaster band is wider.

Results come in four stamps: BREAKTHROUGH, GAIN, SETBACK, DISASTER. As the race gets real (on the ballot, then the general), scrutiny grows and the disaster band widens. There is no pity timer. The dice are the dice. And some offers are devil's bargains — the benefit is real, and so are the strings. The county does not label them. Read the fine print.

The Two Doors to the Ballot

Labor: petition signatures — free but slow, and it eats your weeks. Money: the filing fee — fast, if you can raise it. Fish fries pay for a lot of politics. Most runs die at this door. Respect it.

What the Numbers Mean

  • Contacts — voters you've actually met. The spine of a primary win.
  • Name ID — how many kitchen tables argue about you.
  • Volunteers — make field cards better and pay some costs.
  • Momentum — recent heat. Decays weekly; spend it while it's warm.
  • Endorsements — chairs and clubs. Heavy in the primary math.
  • GOTV — banked turnout, built in the general's final phase. Rapport is a promise; turnout is the promise kept.

Where You Work

Field cards open a ground picker. Rapport banks on the ground you choose. Working the same ground twice in a week is easier but yields half the rapport. Opposition presence shows in the picker — visible, not yet biting your odds.

Shop, Allies, Debt

Shop cards (0 AP) buy assets — voter file, walk list, phone tree — with cash or volunteers. Self-Fund on Credit is real leverage: cash now, a bank note later. Debt never taxes your roll odds; it settles on the win/loss branch (cheap on a win; compounds into the next cycle on a loss). PAC money can bridge a note — then Session will remember.

The Long Game

Allies change the machine — a Canvass Captain gives your field operation a free action every week. Reputations accrue from how you play (nobody outworks you; or the county calls you snakebit). Shadow work — oppo files, whisper campaigns, PAC money — moves your private Faces, and past certain thresholds the county starts telling stories about you that have mechanical teeth.

Losing Well

A run ending is not a reset. The Chronicle records every run as a ballad entry. On a loss you choose how you spend the two years to the next filing deadline — and the trait you take persists across every future run. On a win, the wheel turns: stand for reelection as the incumbent, carrying your machine with you. Lose better. It adds up.
